## Quick Answer

The NELFUND status meanings we can explain with confidence are the ones clearly visible in the public flow:

- educational verification is a real stage
- JAMB verification is a real stage
- email verification is a real next step
- `missing information` is a real visible state

What we should not do is force a meaning onto later approval labels that were not clearly verified.

## What This Guide Covers

Students usually search for `status meanings` when the process feels unclear. A useful article needs to separate:

- statuses that were actually observed
- later meanings that people commonly talk about without source support

So this guide focuses on:

- the status-like stages verified in the public flow
- how students should interpret them
- what a missing-information state likely means in practical terms
- which later status claims should stay unverified

## Start With A Better Definition Of `Status`

Students use the word `status` broadly. Sometimes they mean:

- which stage they are currently in
- whether the form accepted their details
- whether the application is blocked
- whether approval has happened
- whether funding has reflected

The Phase 7.4 research only confirmed some of those layers.

So this article should not pretend to decode every internal label if it was never clearly observed.

## Verified Status Layer 1: Educational Verification

The public NELFUND flow clearly showed an educational-verification stage. That stage included:

- institution selection
- matric number

A NOUN student can read that stage as:

`The system is checking whether my educational identity lines up well enough to continue.`

It does not guarantee success, but it tells you what part of the process is being checked first.

## Verified Status Layer 2: Missing Information

One of the clearest findings from the public flow was the visible `missing information` state.

That matters because it shows the application can stop when something in the record does not line up cleanly.

The safest meaning to assign is:

`The application could not confirm something it expected strongly enough to move forward cleanly.`

The safest meaning not to assign is:

`This always means one specific issue and nothing else.`

The research did not verify that level of precision.

## Verified Status Layer 3: JAMB Verification Stage

The public flow also exposed a JAMB-based verification stage that requires:

- JAMB registration number
- date of birth

In status terms, this means the application has moved from basic educational identity into a JAMB-linked verification step.

This is one reason students should not treat the NELFUND process as a single flat form. It has real stages, and those stages matter.

## Verified Status Layer 4: JAMB Profile Review

After successful JAMB verification, the flow displayed a profile review state. That means the system is not only collecting a JAMB number. It is also checking what that verification returns.

The practical meaning is:

`The application is validating that the JAMB-linked identity data is usable enough to continue.`

## Verified Status Layer 5: Continue To Email Verification

The next visible continuation step after JAMB verification was email verification.

That is the current edge of what Phase 7.4 verified clearly in the public status flow. Anything beyond that should be written carefully.

## What Students Often Want To Know But The Research Did Not Fully Verify

This is where the article must be disciplined.

The Phase 7.4 research did not verify a full official glossary for later terms such as:

- every approval status label
- every rejection label
- every review label
- every disbursement label
- every appeal or correction state

That means this article should not pretend to define a complete later-stage dashboard language if it was not actually observed or documented in the research set.

## How NOUN Students Should Use The Statuses We Did Verify

### If you are still at educational verification

Think about record alignment first. Institution and matric number are already in play.

### If you see missing information

Do not rush into a second attempt. Treat it as a signal that something did not line up clearly enough for the system.

### If you are at the JAMB stage

Understand that the application is now checking JAMB-linked data. This is verified. It does not mean every extra regularization claim you hear is also verified.

### If you reach email verification

That shows progress through the visible public flow, but it is not the same as final approval or guaranteed funding.

## Common Mistakes When Reading NELFUND Statuses

- reading `status` as `approval`
- assuming a visible next step means the difficult part is over
- treating `missing information` as a reason to keep retrying without checking records
- confusing verified public stages with a full verified internal status system
- using a generic social-media explanation instead of the observed public flow

## A Safer Way To Read Your Place In The Workflow

When students say `What does this status mean?`, the safest answer is often to translate the stage into a practical question.

Use this simple reading method:

- if you are at educational verification, ask `what school-linked detail is being checked now?`
- if you are seeing missing information, ask `what did the system fail to confirm cleanly enough?`
- if you are at JAMB verification, ask `which JAMB-linked detail is now part of the check?`
- if you are moving toward email verification, ask `what has been verified already, and what still has not been promised?`

This method is better than trying to memorize a giant unofficial status glossary. It keeps you close to the verified flow and makes it easier to describe the problem if you need help later. It also helps you connect finance progress with other NOUN tasks such as [How To Register NOUN Courses](/articles/how-to-register-noun-courses), [How To Check NOUN Results](/articles/how-to-check-noun-results), and [NOUN Support Ticket Guide](/articles/noun-support-ticket-guide), because the real issue is often not `status confusion` alone. It is usually confusion about what record is being checked at that stage.

## Troubleshooting Status Confusion

### You only want to know whether your application is approved

The Phase 7.4 research does not justify inventing a complete approval-status dictionary. Stay with the verified public stages and avoid overclaiming later ones.

### You are stuck on `missing information`

Treat it as a real verified state, but do not pretend it always has a single universal cause. Review your record alignment first.

### You reached JAMB verification and think approval is close

You may be progressing, but the research did not verify guaranteed later timing or outcomes.

### You want a full list of every internal status label

That was not verified in the research set. The trustworthy answer is to say so clearly instead of fabricating a neat chart.

## Why A Smaller Verified Status Guide Is Better Than A Bigger Invented One

Students often feel disappointed when a guide refuses to define more than it has verified. But on a finance-sensitive topic, that restraint is a strength.

A smaller verified status guide helps because it:

- keeps students anchored to real observed stages
- reduces the risk of acting on invented approval meanings
- makes escalation easier because the student can describe the exact visible stage instead of repeating a rumor
- protects the site from sounding authoritative where the evidence is still thin

In other words, a careful NOUN-specific status article should help students think more clearly, not simply give them more labels to memorize.
